1922 District 17 Conference
From the Golden Anniversary Brochure of Rotary Club of Muskogee, Courtesy of The Rotary International Archives.

Forty-six years ago on Sunday, March 31, 1922 the world's largest Rotary convention* was in full swing in Muskogee, Oklahoma.

Ten thousand Rotarians and Rotary-Anns were here.

 

On the busiest corner of the town, Third and Broadway, a Pathe News cameraman was shooting every scene and putting the name of Muskogee on every silver screen for millions of movie-goers in theaters circling the globe.

The city was decorated more gorgeously than ever. Including "Welcome Rotarians" signs more than 50,000 gaily colored banners streamed in the breeze.

There were more than 150 sleeping cars in Pullman City in the M-K-T Railroad yards filled to the brim with guests. Hundreds of Muskogee homes were thrown open to the visitors.

 

 

The baseball park at Fifth and Boston was covered over and was known as Rotary Hall, headquarters for entertainment of the big affair. It seated enough people that George Wilmarth, Oklahoma Gas and Electric Company District Manager, who headed the mammoth event, invited the entire citizenship to join with delegates in enjoying goings-on.

Walter G. Gibbons who had charge of automobiles which conveyed sojourners had given orders that no conventioner be allowed to walk. Automobile headquarters were at Sixth and West Okmulgee. Virtually every family owning a car in the city volunteered an auto and driver. All were called to duty at 6:30 a.m. the first morning of the convention. Gibbons insisted the city not be caught asleep when the first of many long trains pulled in the third largest town in Oklahoma.

J. B. A. Robertson, then Governor of the State and Ross Burns, District Governor of Rotary, gave opening addresses to 82 clubs which comprised a four-state area of Missouri, Kansas, Oklahoma and Arkansas. Some half-dozen or more clubs from another state made themselves welcome although they did not belong in the Rotary district proper.

Miss Alice Robertson, first woman congressman in America, and a Muskogeean who came here as a missionary to Indians, talked by long distance telephone to delegates and guests assembled in Rotary Hall.

Jerre Locke, Chairman of the Information Committee, had hundreds of Boy Scouts on streets, bearing signs on uniforms "At Your Service." N. A. "Nate" Gibson was chairman of a committee which prepared elaborate programs of the convention. R. J. Tighe, then City Superintendent of Schools, prepared subjects to be discussed by club men at round tables. R. P. Harrison, then city manager, had charge of the "Eats Uneed," a feed personified at Rotary Hall. A. J. Calhoun, president of the club's board of directors, W. F. Moffatt, a past-president, and R. M. Jacks secretary, were assigned to do what was termed "the dirty work" of the executive committee for the conclave.

Wilmarth promised his Mardi Gras, which was staged on such an extensive scale that six of the biggest buildings at the Fair Grounds and a tent were required to house it all, would be the talk of the Rotary World. Each club brought its bands, orchestras and entertainers. Independence, Kan., brought a band of 105 pieces. Clinton, Missouri brought the most colorful aggregation of musicians, according to Muskogee Daily Phoenix.

Harry Ogden, Charles Hulquist, Sam Locke, Stanley Shelor, Harry Gibson, Tony Miller, Otis Graham, Walter "Bobble" Roberts, Paul Pinson, Simon Wick, Charles Dawson, H. E. Ketcham, Charles L. Dickmann, Tom Gaddy, the Bebbs father Robert and his sons, Maurice and Forest, held important assignments.

The conclave lasted three days, March 30, 31 and April 1, 1922.

 

Kansas, Missouri and Oklahoma were together the total of District 17, Ross E. Burns being the District Governor for 1921-22. Arkansas was in a different District but if they were invited it didn't make it an International Convention as we know it. Even though the newspapers at the time may have called it a convention, it is what we know of now, as a *District Conference.

Cyndi Beck, RI Archives
