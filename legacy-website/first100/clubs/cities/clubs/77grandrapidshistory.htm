THE FIRST HUNDRED CLUBS

THE ROTARY CLUB OF GRAND RAPIDS

CLUB HISTORY

Club # 77 - Established March 13, 1913

 

The Founding of The Rotary Club of Grand Rapids

 

The Rotary Club of Grand Rapids became a reality, March 29, 1913, in the Livingston Hotel in Grand Rapids, Michigan.  It was #77 among Rotary Clubs worldwide, and the second Rotary Club in Michigan.  Since that time, Grand Rapids Rotary has worked with care and diligence to fulfill the ideals of Rotary.  Its members have taken to heart the Rotary motto, "Service above Self", and the Four Way Test that has been accepted outside the Rotary organization as well as within it.

 

During the winter of 1913, three Grand Rapids men became interested in the idea of Rotary.  Attracted by both the business and social aspects of the organization, they began to talk of forming a Grand Rapids club and they invited two members from the Detroit Rotary (the first club in Michigan) to meet with them and guide their organization.  Joined by four new recruits, the group of seven agreed to form a local Rotary club and elected a slate of officers.  Hugh Wilson, a banker, was elected president.  By April 13, membership had grown to 16 and their newly elected secretary, William Ames, an insurance salesman, wrote to Rotary International requesting a charter for the Grand Rapids group.  On May 3, a response was received, noted that 25 members were required before a charter could be granted. 

 

Undaunted, the Grand Rapids men worked throughout the next six weeks to increase membership.  At a meeting on July 23, 28 members were in attendance.  Two days later, Ames received word from RI's general secretary, Chesley Perry, that the club had been provisionally elected, and with membership requirements met, full acceptance into Rotary International was being accepted.  On August 1, 1913, the Grand Rapids club received its charter as the 77th club in Rotary International and the second in Michigan.

 

At the turn-of-the-20th century, Grand Rapid was internationally known for its furniture craftsmen.  Industry had replaced agriculture as the principal employer and workers congregated in cities, for that was where the jobs were, but the growth of cities brought forth a brand new set of social ills.  By 1913, the furniture industry was undergoing a gradual decline in its dominance over the Grand Rapids economy.  On the heels of all this change came the genesis of the notions that individuals bore some responsibility for remedying social problems.

 

Whatever their occupations, the founding members of the Grand Rapids Rotary were successful and hard-working men who approached public service with the same commitment and determination that characterized their business endeavors.  Not only did the founders devote much time and energy to Rotary service activities and community fund drives, but many of them were equally willing to play a leadership role in a wide variety of other charitable and civil causes. Throughout the history of the Grand Rapids Rotary, the club's membership roster has read like a virtual Who's Who of the city.

 

The First Community Service Project

 

During the first 89 years of its history, the local club has undertaken a variety of service projects; all of them accented with personal friendship and good humor.  Local service projects have always fit into the guidelines established by Rotary International.

 

The first documented club-wide service activity occurred in 1917 while much of the world was at war.  At a time when the cavalry was still a very real arm of the military and a good horse was a significant asset to a calvaryman, the Grand Rapids Rotary began a fund drive to donate a saddle horse to the armed services.  Much of the club's earliest service work was devoted to helping the war effort.

 

In the area of service to the community we live in, Rotary and Rotarians have been outstanding in leadership roles in practically all community activities.  The Club has taken the lead in extending the concepts and ideals of Rotary to various other areas of Kent County and the State of Michigan.  The Club has sponsored, developed, and encouraged eight other Rotary clubs in the immediate area.

 

The Legacy of Service

 

During the past 89 years the club has been led by many very committed Rotarians, including our first woman president, Nancy Skinner, who served as president from 1993-1994.  One on the list would be Paul Bond who was elected District Governor in 1932.  He was a Rotary speaker over 367 times and maintained a perfect attendance record at Rotary meetings stretching over 60 years.  Member Al Hammer joined the club in August 1913 and two years later he became secretary.  He served in this capacity for the next 56 years until his death in 1971.  This long service in Rotary established a world record as service club secretary.  These are just a few of the many outstanding people who have belonged to and served the Rotary Club of Grand Rapids.

Throughout its history, a list of active and distinguished members of the Grand Rapids Rotary Club would include probably hundreds of well-known men and women, including former President of the United States, Gerald R. Ford, an honorary member for 49 years beginning with his years as a Member of Congress from the 5th District of Michigan.  He has addressed the club some eleven times.

 

Within the local club, the members continually assist one another in achieving their goals in various directions of service.  One example of an activity of the local club is Indian Trails Camp.  The camp has become the special project of the Rotary Club of Grand Rapids.  The club raised from within its own membership and from foundations or other funds controlled by or known to Rotarians, $83,000 in 1957 to enlarge the capacity and provide living quarters for campers and staff.  Over the past 45 years, the Rotary Club of Grand Rapids has been involved raising hundreds of thousands of dollars for the camp.  Just recently, Club members, many of whom sit on the Camp's Board of Directors, were instrumental in helping raise $3,000,000 to upgrade the camp to a state-of-the-art facility for physically challenged children and adults.

 

In 1958, the Club created a Distinguished Service Award given each year to the local citizen who had distinguished himself in service to the entire community, state and nation.  This award now includes women. Three recipients are selected annually and they are bestowed the prestigious Paul Harris Fellowship.  Our own Rotarians have dominated the list.

 

Also in 1958, the Club established the Student Rotarian program in which 40 local high school juniors and seniors from each of ten local high schools are invited to attend bi-weekly Rotary meetings.  The faculty and administration of the schools recommend the students.  The advantages of the weekly interchange, both to the students and the club, have become obvious.  The program has now been renamed the Interact Club and continues to this day.

 

Through the Rotary Club of Grand Rapids Charities Foundation, whose primary purpose is to raise funds to help support programs for challenged youth, the Club supports STRIVE, a scholarship and job program for high school seniors which recognizes and rewards academically at-risk high school seniors.  The Foundation awards up to fifteen-$1,500 scholarships annually.

 

In the field of International Service, many of our members have traveled widely, attended meetings in Rotary Clubs in various lands, maintained contact with people in those clubs and thus contributed to international understanding.  A notable member is Dr. William E. Sprague, internationally known for his work with Rotary International's PolioPlus program.  Dr. Sprague has traveled to over 30 countries as a volunteer healthcare professional putting service above self for over 30 years.  He most recently served as a volunteer for Rotary International, W.H.O., U.N.I.C.E.F and the  C.D.C as an advisor for NIDS for Polio eradication in the war-torn countries of Southern Sudan, Somalia and Afghanistan.  Dr. Sprague has been honored with numerous awards including Rotary's highest honor, the "Exemplary Rotarian" award, granted for outstanding performances in the Four Avenues of Service; Club, Vocational, Community, and International.  In 1990, Dr. Sprague was bestowed the First Annual Mickey Leland Award for International Service by the National Council for International Health, a consortium including Rotary International, the Peace Corps, and the American Medical Association.  The American Medical Association also honored Dr. Sprague its coveted Benjamin Rush Award for Citizenship and Community Service.

 

In the field of vocational service our club has contributed several times to the Rotary International Foundation which offices to college graduates a year of study at any university of their choice in any foreign country with all expenses.  One of the notable of those taking advantage of the program was Guy VanderJagt of Cadillac, Michigan, who later became a member of the U.S. Congress.

 

Through the Rotary Foundation and District 6290's Group Study Exchange program, the Grand Rapids Club entertains groups of Rotarians from other countries to assist in spreading the idea of world peace throughout the world.  Usually these guests are lodged and entertained in the homes of local members.

 

This indeed is only a summary of the high points of the Rotary Club of Grand Rapids.  But from it emerges a theme, which might be summarized like this: It is very difficult to refuse a fellow Rotarian when he asks for help on a project that fits the ideals of Rotary.1
