THE DISTRICT 4590, BRAZIL  HISTORY

A Part of Our History of Rotary in South America

Prepared by the district and not verified by Rotary Global History

"History of the Rotary Club in Brazil" by Alberto Olavo de Carvalho (an extract; webmaster)

The biggest threat to the formation of Rotary in Brazil, was without a doubt, the decree-law 330, 18 April 1938 Presidency of the Republic, which prohibited "organize, create or maintain societies, foundations, companies, clubs and any establishments of a political, but they are ultimately unique to advertising or distribution among their compatriots of ideas, programs and standards of action ... the same prohibition extends to branches ...". The first impact of this decree-law was the disastrous possibility that they were forced to close down all activities Rotary Clubs then existing. With effort of the Rotary Club of Rio de Janeiro and goodwill of the Minister of Justice, then, we were able to circumvent the situation by replacing the statutes of Rotary Clubs, the word "membership" (the Rotary International) by "cooperation".

From 1931, therefore, rose again the enthusiasm for expansion, with rare and spaced oscillations. Today we are the third largest Rotary country ~ outweighed only by the United States and Japan

THE BRAZILIAN DISTRICTS
The Administration System of Rotary International, dividing the world into Districts or territories encompassing a number of Rotary Clubs, dates back to the Convention of 1915 in San Francisco - California. Obviously, at the time of the founding of the first clubs in South America they were not districted. Only in 1927 were created two districts with the Rotary Clubs of Ibero-America.  They were Brazil, Uruguay and Argentina grouped in District 63, during the Rotary years 1927/28 and 1928/29 respectively, the governor of Uruguay and Argentine was Donato Caminara Cupertino Campo Dei.

In the year 1929/30 Brazilian Rotary Clubs composed District  72 covering the whole country. This position remained unchanged under the responsibility of one governor each year until 1938, when the district was divided into 4 sections under the general administration of a governor and collaboration of three assistants. Each of them accounted for one of the sections. Under the primitive conditions of that time, was not possible a satisfactory assistance to clubs, most distant and unable to communicate effectively with each other. It is therefore easy to imagine the benefits of creating sub-divisions under the control of "Assistant Governors".

While therefore inappropriate in this brief report, to enumerate all the District Governors, let me pay tribute, citing the names of those who most stood out among the first ten: Edmundo de Carvalho 1929-30), the Rotary Club of Sao Paulo; Miguel Arrojado Lisbon (1930-31), Samuel Leon Augusto de Moura (1931-32), the Rotary Club of Santos; Lauto Borba (1933-34) of the Rotary Club of Recife; Armando de Arruda Pereira (1935 / 36) of the Rotary Club of Sao Paulo Lins; and Luis Dias (1938-39), the Rotary Club of Recife, aided by 3 assistants.

From the year 1939/40 the four sections of District 72 district began to form themselves in Districts 26, 27, 28, 29. In 1943 the district 28 is dissected, adding to Brazil the District 41. The developments have taken place as we increased the number of Rotary Clubs.

In 1944, there were already six districts, in 1947, seven: 26, 27, 28, 29, 41, 43 and 72, which remained still during 1948/49.

In 1949 Rotary International identified the districts changing the numbers but continued with seven districts under the numbers: 117, 118, 119, 120, 121, 123 and 124, by the year 1951.

In 1952, new unfolding and we have to be eight districts; in 1954 nine; in 1956 ten; in 1958 12; from the previous year with another series of numbers: the move to four hundred hundred.  

After eleven years, in 1969, continued to be the unfolding: we passed fourteen districts. In 1970, fifteen; in 1971, eighteen; in 1973, nineteen; in 1974, twenty; in 1977, twenty-two, in 1981, twenty-four, in 1985, twenty-six, in 1986, twenty-seven, in 1988 , twenty-eight, in 1989 twenty-nine, in 1990, thirty-one, in 1991, thirty-four and in 1992, thirty-six, which two years ago, received at the end of the numbers, a zero, rising thus the numerical designation of thousands.  So we are today holders of 45.57% of Rotáry districts in the region of SACAMA. (South A merica CentralAmerica Mexico Spanish Caribbean. ed)  

BRAZILIAN DIRECTORS
In 1957 Brazil has already occupied the 3rd place in the world in number of Rotary Clubs. However, only 3 Brazilians had so far served on the Board of Directors of Rotary International, while Canada and England, placed below, for years maintained a permanent director.

On the other hand, occupying the first place in SACAMA (over 34% of the entire region), the other participating countries of the region had been twenty-one directors. Because within SACAMA, the criterion of discriminating appointment of director by the Board of Directors of Rotary International.  The fellow Nikola Filizola, engineer, the Rotary Club of Sao Paulo, has prepared, then, a deeply persuasive work, based on statistical data undisputed, demonstrating the inequity of treatment given to the Brazilian Rotarians, finishing by suggesting the zoning for the indication of SACAMA rotating of directors. Working with the numbers of clubs, Rotarians and districts, concluded that the region would be divided into three zones and that Brazil participate in two of them: the south (zone I) with Argentina and Uruguay and in the centre (zone 2) with Paraguay, Peru, Chile and part of Bolivia.

While we have achieved, after extensive work of spreading and conviction of the work of Nicholas Filizola throughout Latin America, by itself, Eurico White Ribeiro, Adalbeno Bueno Neto and others, approve the zoning of SACAMA, we could only indicate Director through Selection Committee of the Convention of 1962, in Los Angeles.

Subsequently we have yet to work on Filizola, isolate Brazil in the zone 2 from three areas. Later, in 1972, succeeded in dividing the SACAMA in five areas, leaving Brazil to the numbers 2 and 4. Thus, we had a permanent director for four years in each quinquênio.  In the last Council of Legislation in 1991, we, after all, six areas for SACAMA Brazil and the holder of three of them: the zones 2, 4 and 6.
