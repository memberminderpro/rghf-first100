THE HISTORY OF ROTARY DISTRICT 7770

(USA)

From the book, A History of Rotary District 7770 in South Carolina, published April 2005

Submitted by

Rodger E. Stroup, Director
S.C. Department of Archives & History
8301 Parklane Road
Columbia, SC 29223-4905

District 7770 is one of the largest districts in the Rotary world. It is well known and highly respected for the quality results achieved by its clubs and members. Today, our district consists of 77 Rotary clubs with a membership of nearly 5,000 Rotarians in the eastern half of South Carolina.

Rotary International has organized Zones and Districts to assist in administering to the nearly 32,000 Rotary clubs located worldwide. Rotary Zones exist to help district governors promote Rotary goals and become effective managers of their districts after their week of initial training months earlier. Our district is in Zone 33 which includes 13 districts from West Virginia to South Carolina. Current governors, incoming governors, and past district governors meet and interact at a Zone Institute each year for several days of training and development, and to hear RI leaders review goals and objectives. 

Our district encompasses the 25 eastern counties of South Carolina (the remainder of the state is in Rotary District 7750.). Our beginning started in 1916 when the Rotary Club of Columbia became the first club in the district. Since then we, along with many districts, have had numerous changes in both reported size and numerical designation to accommodate Rotary's phenomenal growth. The Rotary Club of Orangeburg provided the following: “In the early days of Rotary, the states of NC, SC, and VA formed District 4, later changed to District 7 at which time the Rotary Club of Orangeburg was chartered. District 7 was changed to District 38, and then Western NC and all of SC were made District 58. In 1945 all of SC became District 190 changing to District 282 in 1949. In 1955 the western portion of SC retained District 282 and the eastern portion of SC became District 283. In 1957 District 282 became District 775 and District 283 became District 777. District 777 was made up of the 25 eastern counties plus a portion of Lexington County (in which the clubs of Cayce and Lexington are situated). District 777 was changed to District 7770 in 1991-92 and remains such today.”

Rotary districts worldwide rally to help each other in crisis situations.  For example, during a devastating drought in 1986, Rotarians here and in Ohio arranged for the shipment of tons of hay to South Carolina to assist our farmers in feeding their cattle. When Hurricane Hugo devastated many parts of our district in September 1989, the district issued a worldwide appeal through Rotary channels for assistance. Two hundred and fifty-six thousand dollars were sent by Rotarians around the world, including strong support from Rotarians within our district. We are also generous to other districts and countries  in need around the world, such as the devastation experienced by Rotary districts in Florida from the four hurricanes in 2004, and the Tsunami disaster in late December 2004.

District Organization

Organization and the management of key functions are important to an efficient and effective district. Our current district staff includes the following positions: Governor, Secretary, Treasurer, Finance Committee, Parliamentarian, Historian, Communications (Newsletter, Public Relations), Webmaster,  Membership, Avenues of Service, World Community Service, Family of Rotary, Youth Programs, Literacy, District Training, and District Conference. The staff positions for the very important Rotary Foundation Committee are: Chairperson, Annual Giving and Major Donors, Permanent Fund, International Service, Grants, Ambassadorial Scholarships, Group Study Exchange, and Polio Eradication.

Another important district organization is our 13 member Long Range Planning Committee. It is composed of selected club presidents, assistant governors, and past, current and future governors.

Meetings take place several times each year to examine past and current issues/results. The committee then  recommends changes for improving  club and district operations in succeeding years.

Serving as a critical link between the district governor and club leaders are the 14 Assistant Governors (AG’s). Formerly designated as Area Representatives, AG’s were authorized in 1997 under RI’s District Leadership Plan to handle the oversight duties of the district governor regarding club administration. This change assured the governor more time to oversee other Rotary/district programs and activities, and to devote more time to clubs and programs needing assistance. Each AG is assigned to one of the 14 geographical areas; each area contains 3–8 clubs. In July 2005, the number of AG’s and areas will increase to 16, each with 4-6 clubs.

Our district has produced many outstanding Rotary leaders who continue to serve after their year as governor has ended. As an advisory body to the district governor, past governors (PDGs) meet several times each year as a Council, and remain involved in all major issues facing our district. Today, many PDGs continue to hold district level positions, serve at the Zone level, and hold important positions in top RI programs. We are most proud of two Past District Governors, J. “Lambo” Schwartz and David M. Michaux III, both from the Rotary Club of Myrtle Beach, who have served on the Board of Directors of Rotary International.  

Top Rotary and District Programs and Projects

Not noted but just as important are the wide variety of special projects and humanitarian programs initiated and conducted by district clubs.

 

·        Through  The Rotary Foundation,  the  Rotarians  of  our district  have  been  generous  to  the

world’s disadvantaged people. Today, we are one of the top Rotary districts in the world in support of the Foundation. During 2003-04 we contributed over $733,000 which placed us 17th in the Rotary World (Included is $580,000 in Annual Funds which placed us 4th in all of Rotary.). During the initial PolioPlus drive in 1987-1988, our district raised enough money to inoculate the nation of Peru. From 2002-2005, district Rotarians contributed an additional $520,000 to the final Polio Eradication push. Also in 2002 we donated $50,000 to become a Pioneer Rotary District in support of the Rotary Peace Scholarship Program. Over the years our contributions have made it possible for us to award several ambassadorial scholarships to university students to study abroad, and to send and host GSE (Group Study Exchange) teams with districts in other countries. Humanitarian grants allow clubs and districts to pool their dollars and apply for matching grants from the Foundation. Partnering Rotary clubs in countries receiving these funds involve their members in fulfilling the purpose of the grant.

Much of these funds come from individual Rotarians. A Rotarian who donates $100 per year is known as a Rotary Foundation Sustaining Member. When as least $1,000 is given, the donor is recognized as a Paul Harris Fellow.  As of July 2004, 4,506 individuals have been recognized as Paul Harris Fellows in our district. A new giving category, Paul Harris Society, has already recognized over 50 Rotarians who have pledged to give $1,000 annually to the Foundation.  Our Rotarians also give to the Foundation’s endowment fund by gifts of capital assets and bequests.

 

·        Annually,  our   clubs   buy  and   distribute  dictionaries   to  every  3rd  grade  student  in  our communities.

 

·        District 7770 has adopted the Gift of Life program where impoverished children from around the world come to leading South Carolina medical institutions for life-saving/altering surgery. Most expenses are donated, especially by hospitals and medical staff. We help cover other expenses and provide host families, interpreters, and Rotary club support. To date our district, with generous contributions from high school Interact clubs, has raised over $300,000 for this program thus saving or improving the lives of over 80 children. Recently, we joined Gift of Life International - a growing group of many Rotary districts with GOL programs, and we plan to change our name to Gift of Life of South Carolina.

 

·        In  1994   the  district   created  a  drug  abuse   program,   known  as   RIDDE   (Rotarians  in 

Drug Deterrence Education), which was so successful it was showcased by RI at the 1995 Rotary International Convention in Nice, France.

·        Another  major  district  program, Coins for Alzheimer's Research Trust  (CART),  has  raised

over $1.2 million for research to find a prevention or cure of Alzheimer's disease. The program was initiated in 1995 by the Rotary Club of Sumter, and then adopted by our district. It now has spread to all the Rotary districts in South Carolina, North Carolina and Georgia with a population of some 36,000 Rotarians. Because of the annual grants of $250,000 to major Alzheimer’s research institutions, CART has received recognition as the largest source of private funds for leading-edge research to prevent/cure this disease.

 

·        District 7770 has 36 Interact Clubs at area high schools, each sponsored by a Rotary club. An

annual statewide Interact Conference is held each year bringing hundreds of kids together for fun, friendships, and to promote the ideal of service as an important part of their lives. We also have a very active Rotary Youth Exchange Program for high school students. About 40 youth exchanges are arranged each year with Rotary clubs from around the world. Recently the Eastern Carolina Rotaract Club for young adults was sponsored by the Rotary Club of Conway East. It was a joint effort between Horry-Georgetown Technical College and Coastal Carolina University.

 

  • The Ten Star Rotarian Program recognizes individual Rotarians for service to their club and

Rotary. Upon completing an “Inventory of Service” and receiving at least 110 points in leadership and service activities, a Rotarian receives the 10 Star emblem which is worn behind the Rotary pin. The program is endorsed by RI and sponsored by the Rotary Club of Five Points.

 

  • Rotarians throughout  South Carolina  can now  purchase official DMV Rotary License Plates

with the Rotary emblem and motto. Part of the fees for these special plates is returned to the clubs as a donation to The Rotary Foundation and charitable programs and credited to the donor.

 

Celebrating One Hundred Years of Rotary

 

To celebrate Rotary’s Centennial year during 2005, we will make every attempt to reach Rotary’s top goals, and have added our own. These are noted below with the name of the managing Rotarian.

 

RI Centennial Goals

 

  1. Eradicate Polio – PDG Bill Clowney. Pledges and cash exceed $500,000 goal.

 

  1. Increase Membership – PCP Jon Keith. District membership as of 30 Jun 04 is 4,829. Clubs and district goal is 5% net increase.

 

  1. $100 per Rotarian contributed to TRF Annual Fund – PDG Hal Salisbury. Club donations total over $580,000, exceeding the goal of $500,000 for 2004-05.

 

  1. Clubs plan new community project – PAG Charles Young. Most clubs have registered their projects to be showcased and recognized at the 2005 District Conference.

 

District Centennial Goals

 

  1. Clubs and district compile historical data – District Historian Rodger Stroup. Clubs and PDGs have submitted historical summaries for publication in The History of Rotary District 7770 in South Carolina.
  1. Rotarians purchase Rotary License Plates – PDG Ken Morris. Over 100 “Moving Billboards” now on the streets and highways of South Carolina. Clubs with 10% of members with these plates will be recognized at the 2005 District Conference.

7.   District clubs toast the  Founding of Rotary – PDG  Nancy Moody.  February 23, 2005.  Goal

is for each club/group of clubs to have a special lunch meeting on this date.

8.   Rotarians  attend Centennial  Banquet – PDG  Bob Squatriglia.  February 25, 2005,  in  North

Charleston. Goal is for every club to be represented at this “Celebration Jubilee.”

*                    *                    *                    *                    *                    *

Our district will continue to be recognized as one of the very best in Rotary during the coming years. Since the birth of Rotary 100 years ago, our Rotarians have established a strong, continuing legacy for future club and district leaders. The ideal of service and international peace and goodwill was the vision of Paul Harris. It is hoped that each Rotarian will view the history of their district as a foundation for building more successes for international understanding, and for helping those in need everywhere achieve their vision of their future.
