THE DISTRICT 9810 HISTORY

Prepared by the district and not verified by Rotary Global History

District 9810 is one of the youngest Rotary Districts in Australia being officially launched in July 1982 (as then District 981).  The history of Rotary in South Eastern Australia has been detailed elsewhere.  However, it needs to be noted that the need for redistricting was a result of the exceptional success of Rotary in Australia and the rapid growth of clubs in Victoria.   

So, this difficult task was finally taken on in 1978 by Governors Bruce McKenzie and Neal Wickenton.  The story goes that they were deep in discussion on an airplane flying between the International Institute in Boca Raton, Florida and the International Assembly in Tokyo when they were joined by International President Jack Davies who asked what they were doing.  “We’re carving up our State for redistricting” they tentatively announced.  “Go ahead boys and keep up the good work” was President Jack’s reply.  Eventually these high altitude discussions saw the ceding of territory from Districts 979, 980 and 982.   

The new district started under the guidance of Governor Fred Hay.  It vies with District 9750 as the smallest geographic area but like Rotary everywhere in Australia it is full of Rotarians with big hearts and big dreams.  Fred set the pattern for the strong and inspirational leadership in our District that continues today.  In 2006-08 the District celebrated its first international Director (and in 2007-08 Treasurer) Ian Riseley.   

In the history of District 9810 we have seen a multitude of landmark humanitarian projects completed by our clubs and the district itself.  Sadly, some of the first instances of this occurred within our first year with the Ash Wednesday bushfires in 1983.  Much of the widespread destruction in the Dandenong Ranges was right there in our District.   

Many plans and projects to assist were activated in the ensuing months but a special contribution of the Rotary Club of Emerald whose local community was badly affected is still remembered.  Within 3 days they had printed $20 cash vouchers to be distributed by Rotarians and the Salvation Army (who at that time didn’t have cash funds available to distribute).  The vouchers could be used to purchase footwear, hardware, pharmaceuticals, food and petrol from local traders.  The local traders involved placed their faith in Rotary to re-imburse them for the value of the vouchers.  The club reserves of Emerald, the District and other Rotary clubs covered the almost $13,000 worth of vouchers distributed in those early days.  A great example of Rotary responding to their local community but even more significantly, this was an exercise in Rotary clubs supporting each other and the high regard with which Rotary is held in the community. 

For such a small geographic area, District 9810 has had a propensity to transport their Rotarians far and wide raising funds and spreading the Rotary word.  The Rotary Club of Lilydale was involved in a 1988 national bicentennial event – the Penny Farthing Ride around Australia.  Over 16,000 kilometres were covered by cyclist Phil McDonald and the Lilydale team.  The sponsorships for each kilometre covered for the Rotary Foundation Polio Plus campaign raised over $100,000.  

Continuing the historic transport theme, another grand fundraiser has been the Rotary Club of Sandringham Steam Train rides.  This recreated the leisurely transport of a bygone era with trips by steam train from Melbourne to Sandringham and then horse and buggy rides down to the beach.  Funds raised from these events have helped those with less mobility at the nearby Hampton Rehabilitation Hospital. 

Our most notable way to “send your Rotarians where they need to go” is the Rotary Ride for Research Dollars.  This Ride is a highlight of the pre-Conference activities in District 9810.  Sponsored riders take the long way round to conference raising funds and awareness for the Australian Rotary Health Research Fund as they go.  Initially a project of the Rotary club of Boronia, and later of the District, this ride has raised hundreds of thousands of dollars for ARHRF.  It has carried the Rotary and Health fund messages to communities all around southern Australia and developed some seriously committed bike riders within our district.  The Ride and our other activities supporting the ARHRF place District 9810 as the highest per capita contributor to the fund.   

Our contribution to The Rotary Foundation is also a source of great pride in the District.  In 2003-04, District 9810 became the first Australian district to reach the $US100 per capita target set by Rotary International.  This also placed District 9810 in the top 50 contributors to The Rotary Foundation on a world wide basis.  This was a great year for the Foundation starting with the whimsical but very successful Celebrity Bear Auction.  We sold dressed bears from cricketers, footballers, Olympians, artists, entertainers and even politicians.  There are now some 70 very cute and very collectible teddy bears telling Rotary’s story to children in and beyond our district.  

Also at district level, the “Draw a child’s smile” project in 2004-05 was possibly our greatest effort in quantity terms with the collection of over half a million pencils, pens and chalk which were sent to the children of Zimbabwe.   

Sometimes Rotary is talked of in terms of public conveniences and local park rotundas.  And yes, in District 9810 we have done our share of such structures.  However, there is more . . . and talking about District 9810 would be incomplete without mentioning at least some of the significant construction initiatives both here and overseas.   

An ambitious challenge by the small community of Upper Yarra to furnish and equip the yet to be constructed library was taken on by the Rotary club.  The club also assisted with the actual building and the library with its Rotary connections continues to be a source of great pride to that club.  The Ambulance Station at Nunawading carries a large Rotary Wheel and plaque of recognition for the sponsorship and ‘hands on’ work of their Rotary club in the establishment of this station.   

A playground with a difference was the result of a 1999 Children’s Opportunities grant for the Rotary Club of Chelsea.  Using the grant monies and much of their own labour, the club completed a sensory garden and playground for disabled children in their area.  Another significant playground which meets the needs of children with disabilities was also built with an Opportunities grant by the Rotary Club of Croydon.  Special children with special needs touch our hearts often.  This was experienced by the Rotary Club of Mitcham in coordinating a lengthy project that has built an indoor swimming pool at their local school for disabled children.   

Houses have been built and furnished for both disadvantaged children and adults throughout the district by a number of clubs.  An interesting adjunct to one of these projects carried out by the Rotary Club of Oakleigh, was the commissioning of a video “Don’t judge too quickly” discussing the issues surrounding the growing number of homeless people within our communities.  This video was distributed to all secondary schools in Victoria.  

Perhaps one of the most significant projects involving buildings, and this one in an international arena, has been the establishment of the East Timor Roofing Company (more lately known as the East Timor Roofing and Training Co-operative). 

The East Timor Roofing Company was a project that evolved out of the identification of the desperate need for roofing in the devastated areas of East Timor following their gaining of Independence.  The Rotary Club of Doncaster became aware of this need and the need for work for the demobbed local freedom fighters.  With support from a knowledgeable former Rotarian, the club sourced a corrugating machine, arranged for its renovation and had it despatched to East Timor. The rest is part of a wider Rotary Global History. This project has been supported by Rotarians from around Australia and is another tangible example of Rotary working to promote peace using vocational service as an avenue of service.   

Like many Australian Rotary Districts, 9810 is a great supporter of Rotary programs for youth.  When discussing the development of young people, some of the more interesting initiatives in the vocational area should be mentioned.  The Sleath Lowery Scholarship for young opera singers established by the Rotary Club of Cheltenham is one such initiative.  The culmination of the annual selection process for the scholarship finds many Rotarians enjoying a fabulous evening of opera with students from the Victorian College of the Arts.  The presentation evening for the Achievement Workshop is another such event.  This Workshop brings together future business leaders for a weekend residential seminar which ties personal development and business functions and decision making into a cohesive process.  Each year this annual workshop contributes to the growth and development of 50 of tomorrow’s business leaders.   

Acknowledging the tremendous variety and breath of the contributions of all Rotarians in their local communities and in the international sphere is an impossible task.  This short snapshot celebrates District 9810 and the great work of all of its Rotarians. 

 

This history supplied by Denis Hyland, Foundation Chair 2008/11, District Governor, 2004/05, prepared by Juliet Riseley. Aug '08
