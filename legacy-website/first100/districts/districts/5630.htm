 History of Rotary in Nebraska

District 5630

By PDG Diane Finch

Rotary in Nebraska dates back to 1910 when a Lincoln club received the first charter. Omaha would establish its Rotary club in 1912 and Kearney Rotary was the third in the Cornhusker state beginning in 1918. The 1920’s were years of rapid expansion of Rotary, with 10 clubs forming in 1920 alone. Aurora, Fairbury, Norfolk, Alliance, Grand Island and Scottsbluff-Gering were among those first clubs, among others. At that time District designations differed. When North Platte Rotary was chartered in 1920, it was in District 15, which included clubs in Iowa, South Dakota and Nebraska. As more clubs were formed, boundaries changed to make the districts more manageable.

An early Rotary Pioneer in the late 1920’s was Frank Hamilton of the McCook Rotary Club who would serve as District Governor in 1942. In those early days, McCook Rotary was in District 120 which included some 55 clubs with a membership of 1884.  District 120 comprised the entire state of Nebraska with the exception of Scottsbluff County. Hamilton was instrumental in establishing clubs in Indianola, Cambridge, Orleans and Trenton. At that time he was cited for establishing more clubs than any other man in District 120.

District 5630, which had its beginnings in 1937, is now comprised of some 1470 members representing 35 clubs. Although the geographic expanse is vast, running from Grand Island to Sidney and Valentine to McCook, the local Rotary Clubs play a vital part in each community. Together, they are a major force in helping fund the Rotary Foundation and supporting International projects. This year DG Ken Gnadt of the Grand Island Noon club is at the helm.

The first Rotary club in the District was the Kearney Club, chartered in 1918 with Lincoln Rotary serving as sponsor club and C.E. Alison making the charter presentation to John W. Patterson of Kearney. At that time RI thought no club could properly function in a city with less than a 25,000 population base, but because of the urgent intervention of the Lincoln Club, RI consented to admit Kearney as an experiment. So, the Kearney Rotary, with its 15 members, not only became the third club in the state, but also the first service club of its kind. One of the early highlights of the club was a visit from Chief Justice William Taft, who later became a U.S. president.

The second club to charter in District 5630, with Lincoln Rotary as its sponsor club, was Hasting Noon in 1919, with Dr. James Begthol as the first president. For 60 years the club met at the historic Hotel Clarke in downtown Hastings. Now the meetings are at Taylor’s Steak House. One of their outstanding Rotary events of the early 1920’s was the organization of a variety show in the Kerr Opera House. During depression years in the 1930’s the club came close to disbanding. An outstanding alum was Harry Russell, club president, DG and a member of RI board. In 1993, the club chartered the Hastings Sunrise Club.

District 5630 has a strong RYLA and Youth Exchange Program. Even small clubs like Alma Rotary with less than 30 members, often send four candidates to RYLA. Local club meetings are held in a variety of sites, including an opera house, bank building, hospital center and a cattle company. The largest club in District 5630 is Grand Island Noon with  some 126 members; while the smallest,  Oxford Rotary,  continues to carry on with 12 members. Several members of the District have held Rotary International positions.  Distinguished alumni, among others, are the first District Governor Charles Cadwallader of Lincoln and McCook Rotarians, PDG Ben Hormel, who served on the RI board of directors, and former Nebraska governor Frank Morrison.
