THE DISTRICT 7820 HISTORY

From "Under the Northern Lights"

Canadian history at www.canadaclubs.org

Edited or written by Rotary Global History historian PDG Jim Angus

District 7820 Zone 24

Introduction

At the 1915 convention of the International Association of Rotary Clubs, the eight geographic divisions were changed into 19 numbered districts: 15 in the USA, three in Canada, and one in Great Britain and Ireland. Area vice-presidents became district governors, nominated by the clubs in the district and elected at the international convention. There were 10 clubs in Canada. Two of the clubs were in the Maritimes – Halifax, N.S, (1913) and St John, N.B. (1914). Charlottetown, P.E.I. was added in 1917.

            Geographically one of the largest districts in the Rotary world, District 7820 is an international district comprising the provinces of Nova Scotia, Prince Edward Island, Newfoundland and Labrador, the Magdalen Islands of Quebec, and the French islands of Saint Pierre et Miquelon. There are approximately 1900 Rotarians in 46 Rotary clubs. The District became international first in 1937 with the addition of Newfoundland and Maine. Subsequent changes in 1949 and 1958 lead to the present configuration.  The oldest club is the Rotary Club of Halifax, and the newest club is the Rotary Club of Stratford, P.E.I. chartered in 2002.

District and Multi-District Projects

Bethune Memorial Ambulance Project

Named after Canadian doctor and Chinese hero Norman Bethune, the Bethune Memorial Ambulance Project was a district project created to provide two ambulances for two hospitals in Beijing, China. Originating with a proposal from the Rotary Club of Yarmouth, Nova Scotia, the project was adopted by District 7820 at the 1998 district conference and 22 district clubs signed on. Donations were also received from four other Canadian clubs, three other Canadian districts, two districts in the USA, including a major donation from Alaska in District 5010, District 3450 in Hong Kong, and a major donation from the Beijing Rotary Friendship Group (now the provisional Rotary Club of Beijing). Grants were received from the Rotary Foundation and the Canadian Rotary  Committee for International Development (CRCID).

            The project took almost four years and was completed in December 2001. The Provisional Rotary Club of Beijing held a presentation ceremony to hand over the two ambulances to the presidents of the two recipient hospitals – the Bethune International Peace Hospital in Beijing and the First Peoples Hospital in Wutai, northern China. Wutai is the region where Dr Norman Bethune doctored and where he died.

            It is believed that the project contributed in a small way to convince Chinese authorities of the benefit of Rotary ideals and led to official recognition of Rotary in China. After more than 50 years the Rotary Clubs of Beijing and Shanghai have been granted provisional status.

The Christmas Card Project

The Christmas Card Project was launched by PDG Trevor Atkin in 2002 to raise funds for Polio eradication. The project raised $18,000 that year with the sale of 21,000 Christmas cards. The project has since become a district project designed to raise funds specifically for PolioPlus. Card sales have now reached the 30,000 level with full proceeds going to PolioPlus program.

Have a Heart Project

A district project the Have a Heart Project was launched recently at a District Conference in Newfoundland. Bags of candy valentine hearts are packaged and shipped to participating clubs throughout the district with promotion kits. Rotarians give the bags of candy hearts away to the public on Valentines Day in return for donations.

            The project originated in 2001 from an idea of Rotarian Clark Paynter of the Rotary Club of Halifax Harbourside. Impressed by how successful a Kiwanis Peanut sale was, Paynter thought Rotary could do a similar project. Seven clubs gave out free bags of cinnamon hearts at the ferry terminals, malls and other location around Metro Halifax and Bridgewater. Because the project was so successful other clubs were keen to join and the project spread throughout the district.
