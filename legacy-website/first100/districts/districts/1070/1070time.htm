"In the beginning, Rotary numbered the clubs in the order in which they became affiliated with Rotary International and they were assigned a charter number based on the date that they were elected to membership in Rotary International. After WWII in about 1951, Rotary stopped using the numbering system that it had before the war. Today's assigned numbers are for financial purposes and are not placed on the charter nor should they be considered for order of entry into Rotary International. After a club is terminated (for any reason) should they be re-affiliated within a year, they would retain their original charter date. If more than a year has passed, then they will receive a new charter date and the old affiliation will no longer count as to anniversary, etc." Cyndi Beck, Rotary Archives

CHARTERING AND THE NUMBERING OF CLUBS

Officially a Rotary Club only moves from the 'interim' (provisional) stage to full membership when it receives its Charter. In the early period of Rotary, clubs had to apply for affiliation to the newly formed National (or from 1912, International) Association of Rotary Clubs. Sometimes because of administrative delays or even lack of knowledge of the correct procedure, some of Rotary's earliest clubs failed to be chartered for several months or even years after their inaugural meeting. Thus, Winnipeg was formed at a meeting on 3 November 1910, but did not receive its Charter until 1912.

A similar situation occurred with the clubs in Britain where news of their creation could take a long time to reach Chicago. Because there was occasionally some dispute about the amount of the affiliation fee, not all clubs joined immediately. This has led to discrepancies in the numbering of the clubs. Thus:

Timeline

February1905 The Rotary Club of Chicago (Rotary #1) formed 23d February.

November 1908 The Rotary Club of San Francisco (Rotary #2) formed 12th November.

February 1911 Stuart Morrow, a native of Ireland and former San Francisco Rotarian, helped form the Rotary Club of Dublin (Rotary #65) on 2nd February.

This was followed by the Rotary Club of Belfast (Rotary #67) (24th July),

The Rotary Club of Glasgow (Rotary #60) (spring 1912) and then

The Rotary Club of Edinburgh (Rotary #62) (September 1912).

August 1911 The Rotary Club of London (Rotary #50) formed on the 3rd August by Arthur Sheldon of the Chicago Club, Rotary #1, and an English associate following a meeting addressed by Rotarian Harvey C Wheeler of The Rotary Club of Boston, Massachusetts (Rotary #7). Wheeler was elected President up to 31st December 1911.

1915 The Rotary Clubs in Britain were combined into a single district, known then as Rotary District 19, the other 18 all being in North America.

April 1934 April 9th. Having travelled by train from Newcastle that morning, President Emeritus Paul Harris was met at Grantham station by Leicester Club President Percy Groves. Although the two had never met, Paul Harris wrote that he felt that he knew Groves because “he had written me so many interesting letters.” His topic at Leicester was ‘The American Experiment’ and he spoke about the way in which the country had developed. He returned that night for a 7pm dinner at The Mikado Café in Nottingham where his speech made a considerable impression on his audience, which included not only Nottingham Club members and their ladies, but many from other clubs in District 7 some of whom had travelled considerable distances to be there. The District 7 visitors included a large group from Leicester who had already heard him once that day (from the book ‘Paul Harris in Britain’ by Basil Lewis).

July 1937 On July 8th President Emeritus Paul Harris, having travelled by train from High Wycombe, attended a District 7 Assembly where he invested District Chairman C Emerson Huston (Loughborough) with his jewel and chain of office at an evening meeting attended by over 200 Rotarians from 23 clubs in the District. Harris was welcomed to the Loughborough Club by the President, Raymond Prince, and by the Mayor, Councillor Rotarian Arthur Lacey. In his address to the meeting Paul Harris told the gathering that if Rotary did not change a man for the better, then Rotary had done nothing for that individual. Before travelling to Manchester the next morning for another inter-club meeting Paul Harris visited the College of Technology in Loughborough run by Dr Herbert Schofield, President of RIBI in 1931 (from the book ‘Paul Harris in Britain’ by Basil Lewis)
