THE HISTORY OF DISTRICT 1260, England
 

A Part of Our History of Rotary in Europe
 

Prepared by the district and not verified by Rotary Global History
 
CLUBS OF THE DISTRICT
 

The first clubs in these islands were formed in 1911 and by 1918 there were 22 clubs organised into 6 Districts all based on County boundaries. The Counties of Berkshire, Buckinghamshire, Hertfordshire, and Bedfordshire were in District 3.  Oxfordshire at that time was in District 2. 

Rotary International Association for Great Britain and Ireland, formed in 1922, was renamed RIBI, as we know it, in 1938. The Board of Directors of RIBI decided in 1923 that, "to guarantee that men of ability were evenly distributed throughout the Districts, there should be smaller units", and the 5 counties mentioned above, became District 9.

In 1957 following dramatic Rotary growth worldwide, RI decided to create a further 500 Districts and in RIBI we were told to add 100 to our numbers thus District 9 became 109, although this number had to be reallocated to us from Mexico and Yucatan.

The growth of Districts continued in RIBI and in 1974 District 124 was born and of the new numbers allocated by RI to RIBI 125 and 126 were then in Iceland. This required more reshuffling within Scandinavia and 126 was subsequent reallocated on the birth of District 126 in 1984/1985 when District 109 was split into two. 

All the Northern clubs went into the new District 126 from parts of Hertfordshire and Bedfordshire. plus some clubs in Buckinghamshire. 

District 126 was formed under Rotary International Authority promulgated on 23rd March 1984, and it formally started to operate on 1st July 1985. Rotarian Bryan Archer was the first District Governor.

The area covered by the District contained major parts of Bedfordshire, Buckinghamshire and Hertfordshire.

The District has had a dearth of celebrities as members if its clubs but there are a couple of things of note.

The Hertford Club provided District 109 with three District Governors in succession, namely George Brighten, Eddie Bayliss and Len Harding. Len ran the approved school where RI past President Bill Huntley was also a manager in his youth. Mike Langley, the renowned sports reporter, (father of William Langley currently writing for the Sunday Telegraph), was also a member. 

One member of the Luton club was Lord Charles Hill, the radio doctor, Also Charles Simeons was not only a Past District Governor of District 109 but was also a Member of Parliament for Luton. He currently lives in Norfolk and is a member of the Holt Club. 

Perhaps one can only say that all Rotarians in District 1260 are celebrities in their own way.

CLUBS OF THE DISTRICT
 

Wolverton Rotary Club was sponsored by the Rotary Club of Leighton Buzzard and received its Charter on 27th July 1959.  From its first official meeting in October 1959 the Club has met on a weekly basis, initially at the Crauford Arms, Wolverton and now at The Cock Hotel in Stony Stratford.  The first lady member, together with her husband, joined in the year 2000.  The Club’s name was changed in 2005 to the Rotary Club of Wolverton and Stony Stratford to strengthen its links with the local community.

THE FAMILY OF ROTARY

We are a fun club and enjoy good fellowship during our meetings and at our charity and social events.  Over the past fifty years we have worked closely with, and enjoyed the company of, other Rotary clubs in Milton Keynes.  Our special link with The Inner Wheel Club of Wolverton has flourished since it was established by the wives of our founder members, receiving its Charter in June 1960.  We have always supported each other at both social and fund raising functions.  We also enjoy the fellowship and support of our ‘Friends of Rotary’.

Wolverton Rotaract was originally established in January 1978.  The Club folded due to lack of members but under the aegis of our Club, and the Rotary Club of Newport Pagnell, it was re-launched in 1981 as Milton Keynes Rural Rotaract.  Rotaract supports many of the Rotary events throughout the District and the annual games evening with our Club and Newport Pagnell Club is always an enjoyable event.

Our Club sponsored the Rotary Club of Buckingham in 1968 which last October celebrated its 40th Anniversary.  We always enjoy the company of our daughter club, most particularly at their annual Christmas Carol Concert.

Worldwide we have supported programmes such as Polio-Plus (eradication of polio), Disaster and Shelter Boxes, Tools for Self Reliance (collections of tools for refurbishment), Vision Aid Overseas (collections of used spectacles) and Shoe Boxes (filling of boxes with household essentials and toys for destitute children).  For the second year running we are holding a joint local Rotary Clubs’ grand fundraising ball as part of Rotary International’s campaign to finally rid the world of polio.

In the early 90’s we became aware of the poor education facilities that existed in the most rural areas of KwaZulu Natal, South Africa and funded the building of a school classroom.  In 2000 we learned, through the Eshowe Rotary Club, of a further need to provide funding for sanitation and water storage tanks for five rural schools.  Taking up the challenge we set out to raise £20,000 with the publication of our own joke book, donations from other Rotary clubs and Rotary Foundation matching grants.  Later we supplied material for a further three joke books raising thousands more pounds through royalties for more Eshowe school projects.  Funds were also raised at local fairs, charity golf days, sponsored glider ‘loop the loop’ events, horse racing nights and social evenings.  We are currently planning further events to fund the building of classrooms and sanitation facilities at a further two schools.

We contribute to Rotary International’s own world-wide charity, Rotary Foundation, helping to send students all over the world to extend their education.  We support the Group Study Exchange scheme for young adults to visit other countries and hopefully in the future will recruit possible candidates for Ambassadorial Scholarships. Locally, we actively encourage young people to attend the Rotary Youth Leadership Award course and give support to their involvement.

CLUB LINK WITH JOURE

Thirty two years ago a Wolverton Rotarian, visited his sister-in-law, who lived in a small town in Friesland, The Netherlands.  He attended a meeting of the Joure Rotary Club and his visit resulted in a link forming between our two clubs. Every other year since then we have made reciprocal visits, staying with Rotarian families in their own homes and forging close friendships. Although the formal link with Joure ceased earlier this year, those friendships will continue for many years to come.

Our fellowship with other Rotary clubs extends well beyond local boundaries.  We participate in various Rotary district social events most notably the annual fun quiz, and always support district conference. Our Club was the first winner of the District Bowling Championships.  Five Club members belong to the Worldwide Rotary Caravan Club

SUPPORT FOR THE COMMUNITY

Annually we arrange a ‘Kids Out’ trip to Whipsnade Wildlife Park for disadvantaged children, jointly organise a Schools Technology Tournament with other local Rotary clubs and run a Young Chef Competition for local schools.  In addition some of our members act as reading assistants to younger children.  We have funded play equipment and pantomime outings and more recently organised a Young Photographer of the Year competition. We have previously assisted a local sailing club by providing two specially adapted boats which allow disabled children to learn to sail at Caldecote Lake, bringing to them a sense of freedom they couldn’t enjoy otherwise.

In the past we set up the Abbeyfield Care Home in Stony Stratford and organised the provision of coaches for trips for the blind and elderly.

Probably the most visible of our annual activities are our Christmas Float collections.  Started in the late 1960’s the Float has raised since then some £135,000 for local charities and good causes.  From its early days mounted on a farm trailer pulled by a tractor today’s float vehicle is purpose designed with modern amplification and lighting.

SUPPORT FOR CHARITIES

The Club is very active in community and international service projects.  Our charitable support started in a small way when one of our founder members asked members to put their pennies into a Charity Box, which is still circulated at every meeting.  Initially collections were used to fund an annual donation to the Peninsula Schools Feeding Association, a Charity which we continue to support to this day.

Over the years our charitable support has grown.  Local charities which have benefited include the Air Ambulance Trust, Willen Hospice, The Ark Charity, Association of Young People with ME, Scout Groups and the Northampton NHS Hospital Charitable Fund.
