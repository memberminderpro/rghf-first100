Rotary Global History

Rotary International District 9710, Australia,

 

District 9710 covers rural and urban communities in some 60 000 square kilometres of the south eastern corner of New South Wales and the Australian Capital Territory on the east coast of Australia.

 

It is one of the most diverse economic regions in Australia with activities ranging from primary and secondary production in agriculture, forestry, fishing, mining, horticulture and light manufacturing industries to service based activities such as finance, business, government, tourism, sporting, scientific, economic, cultural, health technology and educational services.

 

Canberra, Australia's National Capital has the Australian Houses of Parliament, associated infrastructure and world class communication links. A thriving wine industry surrounds the city of some 320 000.

 

Australian icons such as the Australian War Memorial, National Gallery of Australia, National Museum of Australia and the National Library and four universities including the Australian National University and the Australian Defence Force Academy and research and IT development centres and the diplomatic corps to Australia are located in Canberra.

 

District origins go back to 1928 with the chartering of the Rotary Club of Canberra which hosted the Second Conference of Rotarians in Australia in 1929. Two Past District Governors, Sir Robert Garran (1937-38) and Jack Olsson (1977-78) are worthy of mention. On Sir Robert's death in 1957, the Australian Attorney General said "Sir Robert Garran's death removes from the Australian stage the last outstanding personality who could be regarded as one of the architects of the Commonwealth of Australia." It was through Jack Olsson's determination and effort in the late 1980s that Rotary was re established into Poland and Eastern Europe on the fall of the "Iron Curtain".

 

Clubs host Australia wide programs - National Youth Science Forum, Adventure in Citizenship and the Model United Nations Assembly.

 

PP Max Hill and PP Gordon Boag actively participated in the submission to Rotary International to have the Rotary Youth Leadership Award program accepted as a global program.

 

 

Written by PDG John Louttit, Rotary Club of Canberra 15 April 2003
