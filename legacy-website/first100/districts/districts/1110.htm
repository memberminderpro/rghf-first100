THE DISTRICT 1110 HISTORY

Prepared by the district and not verified by Rotary Global History

 

See also Jersey Rotary Club

 

IN THE BEGINNING (See also our history of Rotary in Ireland and Great Britain)

The word 'District' was introduced at the International Convention in San Francisco in July 1915 when the first 'District Governors' were created to replace what had previously been called 'Divisional Vice Presidents'. There were nineteen of the new District Governors, and District 19 comprised the British Isles.

By 1919 there were fourteen clubs in what was then called The British Association of Rotary Clubs (B.A.R.C.) and the Convention held in Atlanta agreed that the Governor of District 19 would be the President for the time being of the clubs within B.A.R.C. It's fourteen clubs were to affiliate with the International organisation at an agreed specially reduced inclusive fee of $10 per annum per club in lieu of a per capita tax. (This caused the annual per capita tax paid by Rotarians in B.A.R.C. to increase from one shilling to three shillings!).

One of those clubs was Portsmouth and this is where District 1110 really began. The 14 clubs were represented by B.A.R.C. by 26 directors (The earlier clubs being allowed two each) and at the Convention of B.A.R.C. held in Edinburgh, it decided, as a war-time emergency, to reduce that twenty six to just six directors, one from each of the six newly organised Districts. District 3 included clubs in England South East and in Portsmouth, Southampton, London and Brighton.

By then several new clubs were formed in our area, Southampton, Bournemouth and Gosport, each with about 40 attending their meetings, and on 19 November 1920 the six Districts were organised into eight new Districts, of which the District 4 included clubs in Hampshire, Isle of Wight, Sussex, Kent, Surrey, Berkshire, London, Essex Hertford, Buckinghamshire, and
Bedford. There was no mention of Dorset, but the eventual District 111 was taking shape.

Attendance was even then a matter of concern and clubs felt that more of their members should actually attend the weekly meetings. The President of the Portsmouth Club, writing in 1921 issue of The Rotary Wheel' magazine said, "Probably in every club there are three degrees of members: those who feel convinced that Rotary has a big future and are enthusiastic supporters; those who feel attracted by the movement, but are not quite sure of their own relationship to it; and those who regard the weekly gathering simply as a pleasant opportunity for meeting friends and acquaintances " - Little has changed since then!

1922/23 saw the end of B.A.R.C. and the birth of R.I.B.I. (Rotary in Great Britain and Ireland), with its own Board of Directors. Much increased activity followed with the formation of many new clubs and a further redistricting became necessary, producing sixteen districts out of the existing eight districts.

DISTRICT 1110 finally started to take shape.

District 4 now became District 11, to include Hampshire, Dorset, Wiltshire, the Isle of Wight and the Channel Islands. Our present day form had now taken shape and no further significant changes were found necessary for the next 49 years (1971/2, Geoffrey Pike's year as our District Governor) and there were many who in 1971-72 were strongly opposed to the loss of Lyme Regis, Bridport, Weymouth, Dorchester and Sherborne to help District 120 to the west (In 1961 all Districts had 100 added to their District number and we had become 111).

Thirty years later, in 1991, all Districts added a 0 to our number, and at last we had become Rotary International District 1110!

GROWTH AND VARIETY. Going back to the years between 1923 and 1972 - As did Portsmouth when they looked west towards Gosport and Southampton, so new clubs continued to look in that direction as they spread the Rotary message. Portsmouth, Southampton and Bournemouth each became launching
pads for westward growth and were the mainspring of the formation of some 45 new clubs over the next 49 years, spreading as far to the west as Sherborne, Shaftesbury, Gillingham, Dorchester and Weymouth, and embracing most of Wessex, the historic seat of government of England.

One strength of the District has undoubtedly been its mix of different types of clubs ranging from the areas of Portsmouth, Southampton and Bournemouth to the contrasting resorts of the Channel Islands, the Isle of Wight, Lymington, Swanage, Weymouth and Lyme Regis, and to the long established communities such as Winchester, Salisbury, Shaftesbury, Dorchester, Ringwood and Sturminster Newton.

Each club has contributed experience of service gained in widely varying communities, industries and occupations.

LIAISON WITH ROTARY INTERNATIONAL IN FRANCE.
With the French coast a mere 100 miles to the South (With the Channel Islands even closer), over a Channel which is a challenge to the many Rotarians who sail, it is not surprising that most of our clubs are linked in one form or another with a club as near as could be found to the northern coast of France.

Some clubs have ventured as far as the Loire valley and beyond to find a partner club.

In 1992 our District Governor Don Short took us to Caen for a conference, which attracted a record attendance of over 1,000.

CONFERENCES.
District 1110 is fortunate to have within its boundaries, as well as those of Districts immediately adjacent, a range of attractive venues.

We have never held successive conferences in the same location! Who would when there has been a choice over the years of Jersey, Guernsey, Shanklin, Ryde, Ventnor, Weymouth, Bournemouth, Portsmouth and Southsea ñ Plus Torquay, Plymouth, Brighton, Eastbourne, Cheltenham and Caen, all within a short distance. The 2008 District Conference is to be held in Cardiff, our first visit to Wales. Conference number of attendees has steadily increased from a one time norm of 450/550 to over 1,000.

SPECIAL EVENTS over the years must of course include the unannounced visit of our founder Paul Harris to the Channel Islands in June 1937, when he visited the Jersey and Guernsey Clubs. Later he similarly arrived, unannounced, on the Isle of Wight, where he visited Ryde, one of the four clubs then on the Island.

In 1920 there was a nine day national rail strike when Bournemouth organised volunteer car transport, including taking a group to Sheffield who were unable to get home by other means.

Our District is unique in that two of its clubs, Jersey and Guernsey, fell into German hands for several years during the 1939/45 War. They were not included in R.I.B.I.'s effective membership!

District 1110 has hosted the R.I.B.I. Conference on many occasions, causing the Mayor of Blackpool to declare that R.I.B.I. clearly stood for either Rotary in Blackpool intermittently or Rotary in Bournemouth Interminably!.

In 1976 the R.I.B.I. Conference in Bournemouth, with a registration of over 6,000, had to be held in three locations, the Pavilion, the Winter Gardens, and the Town Hall, with the speakers moving round to give their addresses at each location. That remains a record.

With its 2649 Rotarians serving in 72 clubs, 13 of which are off-shore, District is in good heart and its District Governor a very busy Rotarian!

(Based on an Article produced by Geoffrey Pike in July 1999.)

The District has produced many "characters" and memorable leaders. Two, John Savage of Southampton Club and Geoffrey Pike from the Swanage and Purbeck Club, served as the President of R.I.B.I., in 1972-73 and 1978-79 respectively.

Geoffrey Pike went on to serve as a Trustee of the Rotary Foundation and as a director of Rotary International.

There are now 75 clubs in our District, but by July 2006 our numbers had reduced to 2520.


Submitted by Mike Kearley, 1110 District Secretary.

 

Posted on behalf of RGHF senior historian Basil Lewis, UK, 23 August 2007
