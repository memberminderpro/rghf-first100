A Part of our History of Rotary in Brazil  

 

When the Rotary came to Brazil in 1923, there were 41 districts in the Rotary world. Despite widespread expansion, these Districtsonly covered the clubs in the United States, Canada, Mexico and Cuba. There was no district number for Rio de Janeiro or for any of the first clubs in Brazil.

Only in 1927 was District number 63 allocated, bringing together the clubs of Argentina, Uruguay and Brazil and, later, those of Paraguay.

In 1929 District 72 was created exclusively for Brazilian clubs which at that time, numbered 12. The first or oldest club in this district was RC Bahia, because RC Recife and RC Joao Pessoa were both reallocated.
This story should give prominence to James ROTH (or Jim as he was known), Special Commissioner of Rotary International in Brazil. He began his expansion of Rotary in Rio Grande do Sul, with the clubs in Porto Alegre, Rio Grande and Pelotas. Here there were people unable to send communications to potential club members because of the general lack of communication facilities. Therefore, earlier attempts by Luiz Salazar, Carlos Silva Araujo and Arrojado Lisbon, all failed. Roth however, was unperturbed and with his strength of conviction, his enthusiasm and his sympathy wwas able to succeed where others had failed.
The first Governor of District 72 in the 1932-1933 year was Carlos Buschmann from the RC of Petropolis.

 The governor of the following period, 1933-1934, was Lauro Borba, in Recife who later shaped the formation of Pernambuco and other states in the Northeast, including Bahia.

In subsequent years, the Governors of District 72 included - Jose Carlos Moraes Sarmento of Juiz de Fora; Armando de Arruda Pereira of Sao Paulo (who was the first Brazilian to be elected President of Rotary International between 1940-1941); Carlos da Costa Ribeiro of Fortaleza; and Jose Nascimento Brito of Rio de Janeiro.

Hosting the Conference of the District in 1937, the RC of Bahia, led by partner Carlos Costa Pinto, fought against dissatisfaction within the district, against redistribution and succeeded in preventing a vote for redistribution.

However, in the same year, the RI Board decreed the division of the Brazilian territory by districts 27, 28 and 29, which eliminated District 26.

The area of Bahia was in District 27, along with Sergipe, Espirito Santo, Rio State, Federal District and the states of Minas Gerais and Sao Paulo. Governors of this district included over the years, Manoel Jose Fernandes of Rio de Janeiro (39-40); Almir Maciel of Field (40-41); Pliny Milk of Petropolis (41-42); Arlindo Light of Bahia (42-43); Imbassahy Ernesto de Melo, (43-44), later President of RI in the 1975 year.

 In the year 44-45, the number was changed to District 43, integrating clubs from the states of Rio Grande, Paraiba, Pernambuco and Alagoas, which came from District 26 and Sergipe and Bahia from District 27. In the 49-50 year, the number of the district changed to 123 and in 56-57, changed again to 455. District conferences which lasted from Thursday night until Sunday lunch with much festivity and convoys of people arriving from far and wide.
The first governor of this new section was Lourival de Melo Mota (44-45)covering the end of the WW2

In March 1968 at the Conference in Lake Placid it was announced that there would be further redistributions bringing the north of the country into District 455. This took effect on 1 July 1969.

The relationship between Rotary and the Catholic Church as it effected our district and the RC Bahia, deservers mention.

The Spanish clergy of the era of General Franco conducted a campaign against Rotary which resulted in banishment under the totalitarian regime.

In Bahai, Augusto da Silva, later cardinal, issued a pastoral letter which said that there could not be charity but through the church and launched an excommunication on all Rotarians in Bahia. This created serious reservations among the Catholics Rotarians and strained relationships between comrades. An invitation from RC Bahia was sent to scientist and distinguished professor Father Torrend to make a speech. Despite being Jesuit, Torrend asked if there might be repercussions with the authorities.

His speech was later made outdoors in the garden of the Club Fantoches of Euterpe.
